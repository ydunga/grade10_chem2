You should know by now that metals are electropositive while non-metals are electronegative.

If you remember from our discussion on bonding and electronegativity, then you will know that if the electronegativity difference between two elements is greater than 1.7 then the bond between the two atoms will be ionic bond.

Instead of sharing electrons - like in a covalent bond. In an ionic bond one atom will lose electrons, while another will gain it.

Click on the electron in the outermost shell of Sodium to see the electron transfer.

Both atoms will acquire a charge (become ions).

The oppositely charged ions are then attracted to each other and held together by strong electrostatic forces of attraction.

Some of the common properties of ionic compounds are:

Ionic compounds have a lattice like structure and often appear in crystal form. Ionic compounds are brittle and can be broken into pieces by applying pressure.

Ionic compounds have high boiling and melting points because it takes a considerable amount of energy to break apart the electrostatic forces between two oppositely charged ions.

Ionic compounds are generally soluble in water. If you want to read more about why then revisit the page about electronegativity and polar bonds.

Ionic compounds conduct electricity in the molten state, but not in the solid state as the movement of ions is not possible in the rigid solid state structure of ionic compounds.
