Before starting this section you should have a quick look at the periodic table again and see where most metals and non-metals are located.

Metals are located at the left of the periodic table, while non-metals are on the right.

As you move from left to right, the electronegativity of elements increases.

As you move down a group, the atomic radius increases and the number of energy levels increases. The larger an atom gets, the more distant the electrons become from the nucleus and the easier it becomes for the atom to lose electrons.

These differences in atomic radius, energy levels and electronegativity determine the reactivity of metals.

Oxygen is a highly electronegative element and most metals will react with oxygen to form a metallic oxide.

The general reaction between a metal and oxygen can be represented as follows:

Metal + Oxygen = Metallic Oxide

Most metallic oxides are basic in nature. However there are some oxides like zinc oxides, which exhibit acidic as well as basic properties. Such oxides are called amphoteric oxides.

Potassium and Sodium react most vigrosouly with oxygen. They catch fire if they are kept in the open, which is why they are stored under kerosene.

Below you can see the electronic configuration of Sodium and Potassium. Both have a single electron in their outermost shell, but Potassium has 4 energy levels, while Sodium has 3.

Magnesium, aluminium, zinc and lead react with oxygen and develop a thin layer of oxide when exposed to atmospheric oxygen.

Iron also reacts with oxygen to form iron oxide. In the presence of water and oxygen, iron form hydrated iron(|||) oxide- more commonly known as rust.

Heated copper metal reacts with oxygen to form a blackish copper oxide.

Metals like gold and silver do not react with oxygen.

From the reaction of metals with oxygen we can conclude that Sodium and Potassium more reactive than Magnesium, Aluminium, zinc, iron and lead.

To figure out the level of reactivity among the remaining metals we can observe their reactions with water.



Most metals will react with water to produce a metal oxide and release hydrogen gas in the process.

The highly reactive metals such as Potassium and Sodium react with cold water to form Potassium hydroxide and Sodium Hydroxide, respectively.

The above reactions are highly exothermic. The hydrogen gas released in the process actually catches fire due to the heat released during the reaction!

Calcium also has a strong reaction with water, however this reaction does not release enough energy for the hydrogen gas to catch fire.

Magnesium does not react with cold water, however it does react with hot water to form magnesium hydroxide and release hydrogen gas.

Other metals like aluminium, iron and zinc do not react even with hot water, but react with steam to form metal oxides and hydrogen.

From these reactions we can conclude that calcium and magnesium are more reactive than iron, aluminium or zinc.

Metals react with acids to form a salt and release hydrogen gas.

Among commonly found metals, Iron and Copper have the lowest reactivity with acids.

One final way to check if one element is more reactive than the other is by observing if a displacement reaction takes place.

A more reactive metal will replace a less reactive metal from its compound.
