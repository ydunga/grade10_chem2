Understanding pH

This section is going to explore properties of acids, bases and salts.

You know by now that you can measure the acidity of a substance using the pH scale.

What exactly does the pH scale measure?

The H in pH stands for hydrogen and p is the symbol for the mathematical operation -log10.

The pH scale basically measures the concentration of hydrogen ions in a solution. The higher the amount of hydrogen ions, the more acidic a solution is.

The strength or weakness of an acid or base is determined by their reactivity with water.

A strong acid will give up hydrogen ions, while strong bases will donate hydroxide ions.

Both strong acids and strong bases can cause chemical burns and should be handled with utmost care.

Below are two examples of acids. The first is Hydrochloric acid- HCl.

HCl has a pH of 3.01.

When HCl is dissolved in water, the hydrogen atoms give up an electron and become Hydrogen ions and Chlorine atoms gain an electron.

The second example of an acidic substance is Nitric acid - HNO3.

Nitric acid has a pH of 1 and is a strong acid.

Next you have examples of bases.

Below is Sodium Hydroxide - NaHO3. Sodium Hydroxide is extremely alkaline and has a pH value of 12.

When Sodium Hydroxide is dissolved in water, it seperates into positively charged sodium ions and negatively charged hydroxide ions.
