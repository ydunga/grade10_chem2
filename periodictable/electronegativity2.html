We've already defined that electronegativity is a measure of the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons.

Electropositivty on the other hand is the tendency of an atom to lose electrons.

The Pauling scale is used to measure the electronegativity of elements.

In the periodic table, electronegativity increases as we move to the right.

Electronegativity and bonding

What happens if two atoms with a similar level of electronegativity interact with each other?

If the atoms are equally electronegative, both will have the same tendency to attract electrons. So the atoms will share the electron and on average it will be found halfway between the atoms.

Such a bond is known as a covalent bond.

This kind of bond would be common between two atoms of the same element. So H2 or O2.

What happens if one atom is more electronegative than the other?

In this case, the more electronegative atom will attract the bonding electron more than the other atom.

The atoms will still share the bond, however the end with the more electronegative atom will now be slightly more negative than the other end.

Such kind of a bond is known as a polar bond.

A polar bond is a covalent bond in which there is a seperation of charge between one end and the other.

Polar bonds form between atoms with an electronegativity difference between 0.4 and 1.7

Water is a polar molecule.

If there is a large electronegativity difference between atoms, then they will form an ionic bond. The atom with the larger electronegtaivity will have control over the electron in question.

You can think of a polar bond as having an unequal electron density distribution.

The polar nature of water molecules is the reason why water is such a great solvent.

The hydrogen side of each water molecule carries a positive charge, while the oxygen side carries a positive charge.

When an ionic compoun is mixed with water, the compound disassociates into their positive and negative ion as they get attracted to the oppositely charged ends of the water molecule.

Metals and non-metals are found on the opposite ends of the periodic table and often form ionic bonds when they interact with each other.
