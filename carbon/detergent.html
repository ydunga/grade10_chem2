Soaps and detergents that you use to wash your dirty clothes are actually made from substances known as surfactants.

Below is the chemical structure of sodium stearate which is a common ingredient found in bars of soaps.

As you can see this is a long chain of carboxylic acid with Sodium at the end.

The carbon end of the molecule is known as the tail of the molecule and is hydrophobic i.e repelled by water.

While the sodium end of the molecule is hydrophillic i.e attracted to water.

The way detergents and soaps remove oil/grease is by forming structures called micelles.

On the screen below you can think of the grey circle as the grease or oil molecule and the pink circles are the soap molecules.

Click on the red soap molecule to see what happens!

When dissolved in water, the hydrophobic tails of the soap molecule form a cluster around the oil molecule, while the hydrophilic end form the surface of the cluster.

This cluster is known as a micelle.

Once the oil molecule has been captured by the micelle, we can easily wash it away with water.
