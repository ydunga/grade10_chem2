Carbon is an extremely versatile element and is used by us in various forms.

In this section you will learn more about the properties of carbon that make it so ubiquitous.

Let's start by reminding ourselves of what the electronic configuration of the carbon atom is.

Carbon has the atomic number 6. Meaning it has 6 protons and 6 electrons. Carbon also belongs to period 2, which means it has 2 shells.

The innermost shell can only have 2 electrons, so the remaining 4 are in the outermost shell.

When reacting with other elements, carbon could either gain 4 electrons or lose 4 electrons.

Lossing 4 electrons
Gaining 4 electrons

Remember that the reactivity of elements is explained by their tendency to attain a fully filled outer shell.

Carbon combines with other elements by sharing valence electrons, rather than gaining or loosing.

Bonds formed by sharing electrons between atoms are known as covalent bonds. Molecules with covalent bonds have strong bonds within the molecule, but intermolecular forces are weak.

The ability of carbon to form bonds with other atoms of carbon, which leads to formation of large molecules is known as catenation.

Carbon can form long chains, branches or rings of carbon atoms to form a variety of substances.

Carbonic bonds with other elements are very strong. This is due to the relatively small size of the carbon atom. This allows the nucelus of the atom to hold on to the shared pairs of electrons very strongly.

Carbon atoms can be linked by single, double or triple bonds.

Compounds of carbon linked by only single bonds are called saturated compounds.

While compounds of carbon having double or triple bonds are called unsaturated compounds.
